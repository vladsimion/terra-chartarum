The Six Bearings

Old maps fail badly when judged by one yardstick. A mappa mundi is not a broken road map; a portolan is not a failed cosmology. Each chart here is taken on six independent bearings, scored from 0 to 8 points - one for each principal wind of the compass.

Note the sawtooth around 1300: the Hereford mappa mundi and the Carte Pisane were drawn within a decade of each other, one near the floor of Witness and one near its ceiling. The Instruments

The Cartometry Bench

One chart on this site can be measured without ever seeing it: Ptolemy's, because his coordinates survive as text. For the other nine candidates on the docket the coordinates live on the artifact, not in text - so the bench takes field surveys: pick control points on any digitization, paste them below, and the same least-squares Helmert fit runs, with displacement vectors and a measurable figure.

Dataset -

One control point per line: name, mapX, mapY, trueLon, trueLat - or paste a complete Allmaps georeference annotation (JSON) made at editor.allmaps.org against any digitization linked in the dossiers. Mind each docket caveat below: a projected map wants its own projection inverted before a single global fit means much.

Control points - strike a city to remove it from the fit

On the Ptolemy dataset - twenty-two positions after the Geographia tradition, rounded, with manuscript families disagreeing by fractions of a degree - the fit recovers the famous diagnosis in one number: Ptolemy's east–west frame runs roughly half again too long, so the whole inhabited world must be shrunk to sit on the ground. Strike Babylon and the Atlantic ports and watch the Mediterranean core tighten - the error is not noise, it is structure, accumulated along his longest itineraries. Longitude was the unsolved problem; latitude, fixed by the sun, behaves far better.

For every other map here, the same measurement needs control points picked on the artifact itself - MapAnalyst and Allmaps exist for exactly that. The literature's verdicts: portolan charts fit astonishingly tightly but sit rotated some 8–10° anticlockwise, the fossil of uncorrected magnetic declination; the mappae mundi resist the fit altogether, which is not their failure but their genre.
